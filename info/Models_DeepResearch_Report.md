# Best Pretrained Image-Classification Models for a Tiny 100-Class Transfer-Learning Project

## Bottom line

For this exact setup, I would start with **EfficientNet_V2_S**, **ConvNeXt_Tiny**, and **RegNet_Y_3_2GF**. EfficientNet_V2_S ranks first because the most directly relevant low-data benchmark I found put it **first among common PyTorch backbones when training data was cut to 1,000 images**, while the EfficientNetV2 paper itself also showed strong transfer to Flowers and Cars under short fine-tuning schedules. ConvNeXt_Tiny ranks second because it was the **strongest overall natural-image fine-tuning backbone** across a broad transfer benchmark and is a much cleaner fit for a 224×224 pipeline. RegNet_Y_3_2GF ranks third because it is **smaller than ConvNeXt-Tiny, 224-native, and consistently strong across natural-image transfer tasks**, making it a very credible “high floor, lower overfit risk” option. If you want one additional hedge against overfitting, **EfficientNet_B1** is the best small-backbone fallback. citeturn15view0turn26view0turn27view0turn23view0turn2view1

If you want the **best 224-native first try**, shift **ConvNeXt_Tiny** to rank 1. The main reason EfficientNet_V2_S is not the cleanest engineering fit is that the official torchvision weights were validated with **384-sized crops**, whereas ConvNeXt_Tiny, RegNet_Y_3_2GF, Swin_T, ResNet50, EfficientNet_B0, and MobileNet_V3_Large all sit naturally at or near 224. citeturn0search1turn2view0turn23view0turn2view3turn2view2turn24view0turn0search2

## Ranking logic

I ranked models by the tradeoff that matters for **1,079 images across 100 classes**: **feature quality first, overfitting risk second, 224×224 fit third, and library maturity fourth**. I did **not** rank by ImageNet top-1 alone. A recent cross-domain backbone study explicitly found that **higher ImageNet pretraining accuracy did not reliably translate into better fine-tuned accuracy**, and that **CNNs beat Swin-family transformers for resource-efficient low-data fine-tuning** across the tasks they tested. citeturn27view0

The closest published analogues to your setting were **Flowers-102** and the **1% Tiny ImageNet** setting. Flowers-102 has **2,040 training images across 102 classes**, which is close to your class count and still very small; the Tiny ImageNet low-data setting in the 2025 benchmark uses **1,000 training images total**, which is even closer to your total sample count. Those two sources are the main reason the ranking tilts toward **EfficientNetV2 / ConvNeXt / RegNet** rather than simply picking a larger transformer or the checkpoint with the highest raw ImageNet score. citeturn15view0turn25view0turn27view0

## Why the top three are the best fits

**EfficientNet_V2_S** is the best evidence-backed shot at the highest held-out accuracy if you can regularize it carefully. Its strongest argument is not just raw ImageNet accuracy; it is that, in the closest published analogue to your regime, it was the **best model in the 1,000-image Tiny ImageNet setting**. On top of that, the EfficientNetV2 transfer-learning experiments showed strong results on **Flowers** and **Cars** while using a very short fine-tuning schedule and light adaptation settings, which is exactly the kind of setup that helps on tiny datasets. The downside is straightforward: the official torchvision weights were validated at **384**, so this is the best candidate on transfer evidence but not the cleanest candidate for a strict 224-only recipe. citeturn15view0turn27view0turn0search1turn48view2turn48view3

**ConvNeXt_Tiny** is the best **224-native** choice. It has more parameters than I would normally prefer for a 1k-image problem, but the cross-domain fine-tuning evidence is strong enough to keep it near the top: it led the natural-image benchmark on most of the most relevant datasets, including **Flowers-102**, **Stanford Dogs**, **CUB-200**, **Stanford Cars**, and full **Tiny ImageNet**. It also avoids the biggest practical annoyance of EfficientNet_V2_S: its official preprocessing is already centered around **224/288-style** usage rather than 384 crops. If you want the model that is most likely to “just fit” your current PyTorch pipeline without changing input size assumptions, this is the safest first run. citeturn26view0turn27view0turn2view0turn49view3

**RegNet_Y_3_2GF** is the best “balanced” option. It gives up a bit of peak transfer evidence relative to ConvNeXt-Tiny, but it is **smaller, cheaper, and still very strong on natural-image transfer**, especially considering its parameter budget. On a 100-class dataset where many classes have only single-digit examples, that tradeoff matters: RegNet is less likely than ConvNeXt-Tiny to win by brute-force capacity, but it is also less likely to burn compute and headroom on unnecessary complexity. If EfficientNet_V2_S feels too input-size-mismatched and ConvNeXt-Tiny feels too large, this is the most sensible middle ground. citeturn23view0turn26view0turn27view0

## Why some popular models rank lower

I deliberately ranked **Swin_T** below the top CNNs even though it is a modern and accurate model on ImageNet. The reason is not theory; it is the transfer evidence. The 2025 benchmark concluded that **CNNs strongly outperform transformers for resource-efficient low-data fine-tuning**, and the Tiny ImageNet 1% results were especially bad for Swin relative to EfficientNetV2, ConvNeXt, and RegNet. For your setting, that is a direct warning sign. citeturn27view0

I also pushed **ResNet50** and **MobileNetV3-Large** down the list for opposite reasons. ResNet50 is still the most reproducible backbone in the field, but recent transfer results say it is no longer the strongest choice for this kind of fine-tuning. MobileNetV3-Large is extremely cheap and easy to run, but its feature ceiling is meaningfully lower, which makes it better as a safety baseline than as the model most likely to maximize bonus-bearing test accuracy. citeturn2view2turn0search2turn27view0

I did **not** promote larger timm-only ImageNet-21k/22k transformer checkpoints such as **ViT-B/16 augreg in21k→in1k** or **Swin-Tiny in22k→in1k** into the ranked table. Those checkpoints are real and easy to load in timm, but they come with materially larger parameter/compute budgets or stronger large-crop expectations, and the directly relevant low-data evidence here still points toward the smaller CNNs above rather than toward transformer-heavy choices. citeturn30view2turn30view1turn27view0
